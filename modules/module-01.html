    Concept

    What a QS Actually Does — and What You Need From This Course

    A Quantity Surveyor measures, values, and manages the cost of construction work. In civil engineering, that means taking a set of drawings and a specification and turning them into a priced Bill of Quantities — the document that tells a client exactly what the work will cost, item by item.

    For a groundworks contractor, QS skills aren't optional. When you tender for work, you're pricing directly against a BoQ. If you misread it, miss coverage rules, or misjudge output rates, you'll either win the job at a loss or lose it to a cheaper (and probably underprepared) competitor.

    This course is not about theory. It's about reading a civil BoQ and pricing it accurately from first principles — so that when you submit a tender, you know exactly what you've priced, what you've assumed, and where your risk sits.

    Concept

    How a Civil Engineering Project Gets Tendered — ITT to BoQ to Submitted Price

    Civil contracts follow a standard procurement sequence. The client issues an Invitation to Tender (ITT), which typically includes the drawings, specification, contract conditions, and a Bill of Quantities.

    The BoQ is pre-prepared — usually by the client's QS — using a standard measurement method (CESMM4 for most civil work). It lists every item of work, with quantities already calculated. Your job as tenderer is to insert a unit rate for each item.

    Your submitted price = all unit rates × quantities, summed across sections, plus preliminaries, overheads, and profit. That total becomes your tender. If you win, the BoQ becomes a contract document and the rates you submitted are the basis for valuing any variations.

    The rates you insert are legally binding. A rate that was too low on day one stays low for the life of the contract.

    Concept

    Key Terminology: Measured Works, PC Sums, Provisional Sums, Prelims, Dayworks

    Measured works — the main body of the BoQ. Physical items of permanent work, each with a unit, a quantity, and a rate column for you to fill in. This is where most of your pricing effort goes.

    PC sums (Prime Cost sums) — a fixed sum included for work that will be carried out by a nominated subcontractor or supplier. You add your profit and attendance allowance on top.

    Provisional sums — an allowance for work that can't be fully defined at tender. Defined provisional sums have a rough scope; undefined ones are genuinely unknown. Both are adjusted during the contract.

    Preliminaries (Prelims) — the cost of running the site: welfare facilities, site offices, fencing, signage, supervision, plant mobilisation, insurances, and bonds. Priced in Class A of CESMM4. Not embedded in unit rates.

    Dayworks — work valued on the basis of actual resources used (time + materials + plant), not a unit rate. Used for instructions that cannot be measured in the normal way. Your daywork schedule is submitted with the tender.

    Worked Example

    The Pricing Hierarchy — Unit Rate to Tender Total

    Problem A drainage BoQ section has three pipe items. Work through how a unit rate becomes a section total, and how sections sum to the tender total.

    Each item in the BoQ has a description, a unit, and a quantity. You insert a rate (£ per unit):

    ItemDescriptionUnitQtyRate
    I.1150mm uPVC pipe, 1.2–1.5m deepm120£48.00
    I.2225mm uPVC pipe, 1.5–2m deepm45£84.00
    I.3150mm uPVC pipe, 3–3.5m deepm30£120.00

    Rate × Quantity = Item Total for each line:

    I.1: £48.00 × 120m = £5,760
    I.2: £84.00 × 45m  = £3,780
    I.3: £120.00 × 30m = £3,600

    Section total = sum of all item totals in that section:

    Drainage section: £5,760 + £3,780 + £3,600 = £13,140

    The tender total is built by summing all section totals, then adding prelims and applying overhead & profit:

    Measured Works Sections
    + Preliminaries
    + Overhead % + Profit %
    ────────────────────
    = Tender Total

    Every rate you enter directly affects the final number. A £5/m error on 120m of pipe is £600 lost — before overhead and profit are applied.

    Concept

    How Errors in a BoQ Affect You — and What to Do About Them

    BoQ errors fall into two categories: quantity errors (wrong measurement) and description errors (ambiguous or missing information). Both carry risk — but in different directions.

    If a quantity is understated, you win the job on a lower-than-expected volume. Under most civil contracts (NEC, ICC), the engineer can remeasure and the contract quantity is adjusted — but only if you've priced the rate correctly. A wrong rate cannot be corrected by remeasurement.

    If a description is ambiguous or missing, you may have priced something different from what the client intended. This is where the coverage rules (which you'll study in Module 02) become critical — they determine what's included in a rate even when it isn't explicitly stated.

    What to do: Before tender, read the entire BoQ carefully. Raise queries in writing via the tender query process. Document your assumptions. If something looks wrong, ask — don't silently correct it and don't price it without flagging the issue.
