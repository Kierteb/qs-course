    Concept

    What Earthworks Pricing Covers

    Earthworks is typically the largest single cost on a civil groundworks contract. It involves moving material — either removing it from site or placing and compacting it to form embankments, cuttings, or pavement foundations. In CESMM4, Class E covers all earthworks.

    A bulk excavation unit rate must include all of:

    • Excavation plant: excavator cost per m³ (hire/ownership + fuel + operator)
    • Disposal: haulage cost per m³ loose (not net — apply bulking factor) + tip gate fee
    • Site management: traffic management, banksmen during loading (if required)

    A filling unit rate (import and place) must include:

    • Material supply: imported fill at the delivered price (converted to m³ using density)
    • Spreading and compaction plant: dumper, dozer/grader, vibrating roller
    • Testing: compaction testing may be required — check specification
    CESMM4 Class E items are in m³ of bank measure — the volume of material before excavation. You must apply bulking factors for disposal and shrinkage factors for fill when converting to real quantities for cost purposes.

    Concept

    Excavator Selection and Output

    Choosing the right machine for an earthworks operation determines both output and cost. Too small a machine and you don't dig fast enough; too large and the overhead cost is wasted.

    MachineBest forTypical Output
    3–5T mini excavator Restricted access, internal work, service trenches in confined spaces 15–40 m³/day
    13T tracked excavator General bulk excavation, trench work up to 3m, drainage 120–200 m³/day
    20T tracked excavator Heavy bulk dig, deep trenches, rock breaking attachment 200–350 m³/day
    Dozer / Scrapers Large open-cut earthworks, pushing over long distances on site 500–2,000 m³/day

    Output varies significantly with material type. Clay at 1.5m depth with good access might yield 180 m³/day from a 13T machine. Stiff clay at 3m depth, restricted access, with dewatering might yield only 60 m³/day from the same machine.

    Always state your output assumption in your pricing notes. If site conditions are worse than assumed, you have documented evidence for a compensation event claim under NEC or a variation under JCT/ICE forms.

    Gate fee: £17.00/m³ net (1.7 t/m³ bank × £10/t)

    The gate fee is charged per tonne of material arriving at tip. 1 m³ of clay in the ground (bank measure) weighs approximately 1.7 t, so the gate fee per BoQ m³ = 1.7 × £10 = £17.00. The haulage cost is calculated on loose volume (after bulking) because that is what fills the wagons.

    Haulage: £13.75/m³
    Gate fee: £17.00/m³

    This is the earthworks rate to enter in the BoQ. It covers excavation, loading, haulage, and tipping in one all-in rate per net m³. Overhead and profit are added later in the tender summary (or built into a company mark-up applied to all rates).

    This rate is sensitive to three variables: machine output, haul distance, and tip gate fee. A 10 km haul might cost twice as much in haulage; a contaminated tip might charge £60/t gate fee instead of £10.

    Concept

    Topsoil Stripping, Cut-to-Fill, and Near-Services Excavation

    Not all earthworks are priced the same. Three special categories have distinct cost profiles:

    Topsoil Stripping

    Topsoil (the upper 150–300 mm organic layer) is stripped before bulk excavation begins. It cannot be used as structural fill and must be separately stockpiled for later reinstatement or disposed of. CESMM4 measures topsoil separately from bulk excavation. Rates: £3–7/m² or £15–35/m³, depending on depth and disposal route.

    Cut-to-Fill

    Where excavated material from a cutting is reused as fill in an embankment or low area on the same site, a cut-to-fill earthworks balance is required. The key variables:

    • Bulking: 1 m³ bank → 1.25 m³ loose for haul
    • Shrinkage: 1 m³ loose → ~0.88 m³ compacted (granular fill)
    • Net: 1 m³ bank excavated yields approximately 1.25 × 0.88 = 1.10 m³ compacted fill

    This means you can fill a slightly larger area than you excavate — but the earthworks balance must be calculated carefully. Misread the balance and you either dispose of fill you needed, or run short and pay for imported material.

    Excavation near Existing Services

    Within 0.5 m of known services, machine excavation is prohibited (or strictly limited). Hand excavation replaces machine work, and output drops from ~150 m³/day (machine) to 1–3 m³/day (hand). This is one of the most expensive operations per m³ in civil work. Always check whether the BoQ explicitly prices this or whether it is included in the standard excavation rate.

    Service corridor pricing: If the preambles include near-service excavation in the standard rate, you must build in a contingency. If it is a separate item, price it at full hand-dig rates.

    Worked Example

    Pricing a Topsoil Strip and Cut-to-Fill Balance

    Problem A site has 2,000 m² of topsoil at 200 mm depth (400 m³ total). The topsoil is stripped and stockpiled on site for later reinstatement. Below that, 800 m³ net of clay subsoil is excavated and reused as fill in a low-lying corner. Calculate (a) the topsoil strip cost, and (b) whether the cut-to-fill balance works.
    Rates: topsoil strip and stockpile £5/m²; bulk excavation (machine only) £5.20/m³; clay bulking 25%; fill compaction factor 88%.
    Area: 2,000 m²
    Rate: £5.00/m²
    ─────────────────────────
    Topsoil strip: £10,000

    Topsoil is measured in m² (or m³ if the BoQ uses m³). The rate includes strip, load, transport on site, and stockpile. No disposal cost because it is stockpiled for reinstatement later.

    Volume: 800 m³ net
    Excavation rate: £5.20/m³
    ──────────────────────────────
    Excavation cost: £4,160

    This covers the excavator only — no disposal because we are reusing the clay on site. Haul cost on site (dozer/dumper moving material to fill area) should be added if the haul distance is significant.

    Clay cut (bank): 800 m³
    After bulking (loose): 800 × 1.25 = 1,000 m³
    After compaction: 1,000 × 0.88 = 880 m³ compacted fill

    Fill needed (say): 750 m³ compacted
    Fill available: 880 m³ compacted

    Surplus cut: 880 − 750 = 130 m³ to dispose

    The cut produces more compacted fill than needed. The surplus 130 m³ must be disposed of off-site. Always work through the balance in this way: bank → loose → compacted. Getting the balance wrong by even 10% on a large earthworks job can mean unexpected disposal costs of tens of thousands of pounds.

    Trench Excavation vs Bulk Excavation: Key Differences

    Trench excavation (for pipes, foundations, culverts) is measured separately from bulk excavation in CESMM4, and for good reason — the cost per m³ is significantly higher.

    FactorBulk ExcavationTrench Excavation
    Machine swing arc Full 360° rotation possible Restricted — must work along the trench
    Repositioning Minimal — large open face Frequent — track along the run
    Spoil management Wagons can spot freely Spoil must be cast to one side; access limited
    Trench support Not usually needed Required >1.2 m or per ground conditions
    Typical output (13T) 150–200 m³/day 40–100 m³/day depending on depth
    Typical cost (excavation only) £5–8/m³ £8–20/m³ depending on depth

    In practice, trench excavation rates are often embedded in the pipe-laying super-rate (a single Class I rate per metre of pipe that includes all trench work). We will work through the full pipe rate in Module 08.
