    Concept

    The Drainage Super-Rate: What Goes Into One Line of the BoQ

    In CESMM4, a Class I pipe item is measured in linear metres (m). That single line in the BoQ — for example, "Pipe, uPVC 225mm nominal bore, depth 1–1.5m: 180 m" — must be priced to cover everything in and around the trench per metre run:

    • Trench excavation: excavator cost per m³ of trench × trench volume per linear metre
    • Trench disposal: haulage and tipping of excavated spoil (applying bulking factor)
    • Pipe supply: pipe material cost per metre, supplied to site
    • Pipe bedding: granular material placed below and around the pipe (as specified)
    • Laying and jointing: labour cost per metre (gang output)
    • Backfill and compaction: material placed above the pipe, compacted in layers
    • Reinstatement: reinstating the trench surface (if priced here rather than separately)

    This is why drainage rates vary so much: a 150mm pipe at 0.8m depth costs fundamentally less per metre than a 450mm pipe at 2.5m depth, even though both appear as a single rate in the BoQ.

    Read the Preambles first. Some preambles exclude trench excavation and disposal from the pipe rate — they are measured separately in Class E. If you price excavation into both, you double-count and over-price; if you miss it, you lose money. The preamble tells you exactly what the rate must include.

    Concept

    Trench Geometry: Calculating Volumes Per Linear Metre

    To build a drainage unit rate, you first need to calculate the volumes of excavation and backfill per linear metre of pipe. These depend on trench width, trench depth, and pipe diameter.

    Trench width is set by the preamble or specification. Typical minimum widths:

    Pipe Nominal BoreMinimum Trench Width
    Up to 150mm0.45 m
    225mm0.60 m
    300mm0.75 m
    450mm1.00 m
    600mm1.20 m

    Trench depth is the depth to the pipe invert (bottom of the pipe) plus the pipe wall thickness. CESMM4 classifies pipes into depth bands (0.5m, 1m, 1.5m, 2m, 2.5m, 3m+) — the band determines the rate band in the BoQ.

    Trench excavation volume per metre run:

    V = Width (m) × Depth (m) × 1.0 m (length unit)

    Backfill volume per metre run = Trench volume minus pipe volume (usually minor, often ignored for small pipes) minus bedding below pipe.

    Trench side batters: In unstable ground, the trench may need to be battered (sloped sides) rather than vertical. This increases excavation volume — calculate the trapezoid volume, not a rectangle. The preamble should state whether battered trenches are measured at the actual excavated width.

    Worked Example

    Building a 225mm Pipe Unit Rate at 1.5m Depth

    Problem Price a CESMM4 Class I item: "Pipe, uPVC 225mm nominal bore, depth 1–1.5m." Preambles confirm the rate includes: excavation, disposal, pipe, granular bedding (Class B, 100mm below and 300mm above pipe), laying and jointing, and granular backfill. Trench width: 0.60m; depth: 1.4m to invert.
    Rates: Excavation (trench) £12/m³; disposal £14/m³ net; pipe supply £22/m; bedding (10mm gravel) £26/t; labour £19.43/m; granular backfill £15/m³ (incl. place & compact). Clay density 1.7 t/m³, bulking 25%.
    Trench volume/m: 0.60m wide × 1.40m deep × 1m = 0.840 m³/m

    Excavation: 0.840 m³/m × £12/m³ = £10.08/m
    Disposal: 0.840 m³/m × £14/m³ net = £11.76/m
    ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Excav. + disposal: £21.84/m

    The disposal rate of £14/m³ is already expressed as per m³ net BoQ volume (the calculation to convert loose volume is done when building that rate). Here we apply it directly to the trench volume.

    Pipe supply: 1m × £22/m = £22.00/m

    Bedding volume/m: 0.60m × (0.10 + 0.30)m = 0.240 m³/m
    Bedding cost/m³: £26/t × 1.8 t/m³ × 1.10 wastage = £51.48/m³
    Bedding cost/m: 0.240 × £51.48 = £12.36/m

    Backfill volume/m: 0.840 − 0.240 − 0.025* = ~0.575 m³/m
    (*pipe cross-section ~0.025m² for 225mm OD, minor adjustment)
    Backfill cost/m: 0.575 × £15 = £8.63/m
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Materials + fill: £22.00 + £12.36 + £8.63 = £42.99/m

    Note the bedding volume assumes 100mm (0.10m) below the pipe and 300mm (0.30m) above, full trench width. In practice, check the specification drawing for the exact bedding zone detail.

    Excavation + disposal: £21.84/m
    Pipe supply: £22.00/m
    Bedding: £12.36/m
    Backfill: £ 8.63/m
    Labour: £19.43/m
    ─────────────────────────────────
    Unit rate: £84.26/m

    This full rate for a 225mm uPVC pipe at 1.4m depth would appear in the BoQ as a single number. If you forgot to include the excavation or the bedding, the rate would be dangerously low. Breaking it into components like this ensures you never miss a cost element.

    Concept

    Manholes and Chambers: Pricing by Number

    Manholes are priced as a lump sum per number (nr) under CESMM4 Class K. Each manhole is a unique item — described by construction type, internal diameter, and depth band. Your price per manhole must cover all work from excavation to completed installation:

    • Excavation and disposal of the manhole pit (larger volume than a pipe trench)
    • Concrete base slab (thickness and mix to specification)
    • Chamber construction: precast concrete rings or brickwork to the specified internal diameter
    • Benching inside the chamber (concrete haunching around the channel)
    • Step irons or ladder (if specified)
    • Frame and cover (heavy duty, medium duty, or light duty — load class to specification)
    • Backfill and compaction around the chamber
    • Connections from incoming and outgoing pipes

    Manhole cost increases non-linearly with depth: a 3m deep brick manhole costs significantly more than twice a 1.5m deep one, because the brickwork is more extensive, the excavation is larger, and the working conditions are harder.

    Common mistake: Pricing the precast rings only and forgetting the base slab, frame and cover, connections, and backfill. Always work through a manhole checklist item by item.

    Worked Example

    Pricing a 1200mm Precast Manhole at 2.0m Depth

    Problem Price a CESMM4 Class K item: "Precast concrete manhole, 1200mm internal diameter, depth 1.5–2.0m: 4 nr." Build the lump sum rate per manhole.
    Components: precast base £420; rings at £210/ring (500mm high); reducing slab £300; D400 frame and cover £230; step irons (included in base); 3 pipe connections at £100 each. Excavation pit: 1.8m dia × 2.2m deep (allow for working space); excavation rate £12/m³, disposal £14/m³ net; labour installation £420/nr; backfill £15/m³.
    Base unit: £420
    Rings: (2.0 − 0.5 base − 0.5 taper) ÷ 0.5m = 2 rings × £210 = £420
    Reducing slab: £300
    Frame & cover: £230
    Connections: 3 × £100 = £300
    ───────────────────────────────
    Supply total: £1,670

    Ring count: total depth 2.0m, minus base (0.5m) and taper (0.5m) = 1.0m of rings ÷ 0.5m per ring = 2 rings. Always sketch this out — incorrect ring count is a common error.

    Pit volume: π × (1.8/2)² × 2.2 = π × 0.81 × 2.2 ≈ 5.60 m³

    Excavation: 5.60 m³ × £12/m³ = £67.20
    Disposal: 5.60 m³ × £14/m³ = £78.40

    Chamber volume (rings): π × (1.2/2)² × 2.0 ≈ 2.26 m³
    Backfill vol: 5.60 − 2.26 ≈ 3.34 m³
    Backfill: 3.34 m³ × £15/m³ = £50.10
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Excavation + disposal + backfill: £195.70

    The pit is sized to allow working space around the chamber. The chamber itself displaces some backfill volume. The calculation does not need to be exact — ±10% is acceptable at tender stage. Use a consistent method and document your assumptions.

    Supply components: £1,670.00
    Excavation + disposal: £ 145.60
    Backfill: £ 50.10
    Installation labour: £ 420.00
    ────────────────────────────────────
    Rate per manhole: £2,285.70

    Round to: £2,300 nr

    This is the BoQ rate to enter for each 1200mm dia, 2m deep manhole. With 4 nr in the BoQ, the section total is approximately £9,200. Rates in this range (£1,500–£3,000/nr for a standard 1200mm manhole) are commercially normal for this specification.

    Concept

    Dewatering, Trench Support, and Drainage Risk

    Two risk factors can transform a standard drainage item into a major cost centre: groundwater and unstable ground. Both must be identified at tender and either priced explicitly or acknowledged as a risk.

    Dewatering

    If the water table is at or above the pipe invert depth, the trench will flood. Options include:

    • Sump pumping: simple submersible pump in the trench — cheap but limited to modest inflows
    • Wellpoint dewatering: a ring of shallow wells around the excavation, pumping continuously — effective for granular soils, significant cost (£500–2,000+/day for system + pumps)
    • Sheet piling / secant piles: for deep excavations in high water table — expensive but provides both support and water cut-off

    Trench Support

    Trenches deeper than 1.2m (or less in unstable ground) require support to prevent collapse — both for safety and to prevent overbreak. Methods:

    • Drag box / trench box: hydraulic steel box moved along the trench as work progresses — most common for drainage. Cost: £150–350/day hire.
    • Steel sheet piling: driven ahead of excavation — used for deep trenches or where collapse risk is high. Expensive and requires crane.
    • Timber shoring: traditional method, labour intensive, used for short runs in difficult access.
    Pricing tip: If the ground investigation shows groundwater or soft ground, price dewatering and trench support in Preliminaries or as explicit additional items. Do not bury these costs in pipe rates — if quantities change, you need to recover these costs separately. If the BoQ does not have dedicated items and the preambles include these costs in the pipe rate, add a contingency allowance or raise a tender query.
