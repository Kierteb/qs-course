    Concept

    Owned Plant vs Hired Plant

    Plant costs in a civil tender come from two sources: owned plant (the company's own machines) and hired plant (brought in from a plant hire company for the duration of the project).

    The costing approach differs between the two:

    ApproachOwned PlantHired Plant
    Cost basis Depreciation + maintenance + insurance + fuel + operator Hire rate (dry or wet) + fuel (if dry) + operator (if dry)
    Flexibility Fixed cost — must recover whether working or idle Only hired for the period needed — returned when not required
    Risk Breakdown cost falls on contractor; must carry own plant maintenance Replacement arranged by hire company; less downtime risk
    Record keeping Internal plant schedule with standard rates per hour Hire invoices — confirm dry vs wet rate before ordering
    Dry rate vs Wet rate: A dry hire rate includes the machine only — you supply the fuel and operator separately. A wet hire rate includes machine, fuel, and operator in one charge. Always confirm which applies — the difference is significant when building a rate.

    Most medium and large groundworks contractors use a mix: key plant (excavators, dumpers) is often owned; specialist plant (piling rigs, compaction plates) is usually hired in for specific operations.

    Concept

    Building a Plant Cost: The Five Components

    For owned plant, or for dry-hire plant where you add individual components, the all-in plant cost is built from five elements:

    ComponentHow calculated
    Depreciation Purchase price ÷ economic life in working hours. A £90,000 excavator lasting 10,000 hrs = £9.00/hr depreciation.
    Maintenance & repairs Typically 50–100% of annual depreciation. Includes servicing, consumables (filters, oil), and breakdown repairs.
    Insurance Plant all-risks insurance, typically 1–2% of replacement value per year.
    Fuel & oils Litres per hour × current diesel price. A 13T excavator burns ~8–12 L/hr depending on duty cycle.
    Operator CPCS-certified operator all-in rate (£26–£30/hr). Add only once — not if already in a wet hire rate.

    Most contractors maintain an internal plant schedule: a standard cost per hour for each machine. This rate is charged to contracts at an agreed hourly or daily rate, regardless of whether the machine is owned or on internal hire.

    Mobilisation and demobilisation costs (loading, transport to/from site) are fixed costs that do not vary with output. They belong in Preliminaries, not in unit rates.

    Worked Example

    Calculating Excavator Cost Per m³

    Problem A 13T excavator is hired dry at £500/day. Diesel costs £1.35/litre; the machine uses 10 L/hr. A CPCS operator costs £28/hr all-in. The machine achieves 180 m³/day of bulk clay excavation. Calculate the plant cost per m³.
    Dry hire rate: £500.00/day
    + Fuel (10 L/hr × £1.35 × 8 hrs): £108.00/day
    + Operator (£28/hr × 8 hrs): £224.00/day
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────
    Total plant cost: £832.00/day

    The dry hire rate covers only the machine. Fuel and operator are added separately because this is a dry hire. If the hire was wet (machine + fuel + operator), you would use only the wet hire day rate.

    Total daily cost: £832.00
    ÷ Output: ÷ 180 m³/day
    ──────────────────────────────
    Plant cost: £4.62/m³

    This plant cost per m³ is the excavation component only. Disposal (haulage and tipping) is a separate cost — calculated on the loose volume (m³ net × bulking factor).

    If ground conditions are worse than expected and output drops to 120 m³/day:

    Total daily cost: £832.00
    ÷ Output: ÷ 120 m³/day
    ──────────────────────────────
    Plant cost: £6.93/m³ (+50%)

    A 33% drop in output produces a 50% increase in unit cost. This is why output assumptions must be conservative and well-documented — output risk directly translates to cost overrun.

    Concept

    Output Factors: What Changes the Rate?

    Plant output is a benchmark — the number of m³ per day the machine can achieve under average conditions. In reality, output varies with site conditions, and you must adjust your benchmark accordingly.

    FactorEffect on Output
    Material type (clay vs rock vs granular) Rock may require breaking before digging — dramatically reduces output and requires specialist plant. Granular sand is fast; stiff clay is slower.
    Working depth Deeper excavation = longer cycle times. Beyond arm reach, the machine must reposition more frequently. Trench work: output falls with depth.
    Confined space / restricted access Unable to swing freely. Frequent repositioning. Reduced swing arc reduces cycle efficiency by 20–40%.
    Waiting for wagons Excavator is idle if the wagon fleet is too small. Idle time costs the same as working time. Size your fleet correctly (see Worked Example).
    Groundwater / wet conditions Slippery ramps, heaving trench bottom, dewatering pumping interruptions. Can halve output in waterlogged clay.
    Existing services Must slow down and hand-trim within 0.5m of known services. Can reduce to hand-dig pace (2 m³/day/operative).
    Document your assumptions: When you set an output benchmark, write it into your pricing notes: "13T excavator, clay, 1.5m depth, unrestricted, 180 m³/day." If conditions are worse on site, you have a documented basis for a compensation event claim.

    Worked Example

    Sizing a Wagon Fleet to Match Excavator Output

    Problem A 13T excavator digs 180 m³/day (net clay). Clay bulks 25%. Tip is 8 km away; round-trip takes 45 minutes. Wagons carry 8 m³ loose. How many wagons are needed to keep the excavator running without idle time?
    Net volume: 180 m³/day
    × Bulking factor: × 1.25
    ─────────────────────────────
    Loose volume: 225 m³/day

    The 25% bulking converts net (bank) m³ to loose m³. Wagons carry loose material, so loose volume drives the number of loads.

    Loose volume: 225 m³/day
    ÷ Wagon capacity: ÷ 8 m³
    ─────────────────────────────
    Loads needed: 28.1 → 29 loads/day

    Round up: you cannot send a fraction of a wagon load. 29 loads × 8 m³ = 232 m³ loose capacity, enough for the daily dig.

    Loading time per wagon: ~15 minutes
    Round-trip time: 45 minutes
    Total cycle per wagon: 60 minutes (1 hr)

    Loads per wagon per day: 8 hrs ÷ 1 hr/load = 8 loads/wagon

    Wagons needed: 29 loads ÷ 8 loads/wagon = 3.6 → 4 wagons

    With 4 wagons (32 loads/day capacity), the excavator should rarely wait. Budget: 4 wagons × £550/day = £2,200/day haulage. This is the disposal cost component of your earthworks rate.
