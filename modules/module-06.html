    Concept

    How Material Pricing Works in a Civil Tender

    Material costs in civil and groundworks tenders are rarely straightforward. Prices are quoted by different units (tonnes, m³, nr, litres), need conversion to BoQ units, and must include haulage, wastage, and sometimes specialist handling.

    The fundamental challenge: suppliers quote in tonnes; BoQ items are measured in m³. You must convert using bulk density, then apply a wastage allowance to ensure you order enough.

    The full material cost per m³ (net BoQ quantity) follows this logic:

    Material cost per m³ = Supply rate (£/t) × Bulk density (t/m³) × (1 + Wastage fraction)

    Each variable must come from a reliable source: supplier quotation for the rate, ground investigation or specification for the density, and your company's standard wastage table for the allowance.

    Always quote on the spec. Substituting a cheaper material without engineer approval can lead to rejection of the work, remedial cost, and potential professional liability. Match the material specification exactly when enquiring.

    Concept

    Bulk Density: Converting Tonnes to m³

    Bulk density (also called dry density or placed density for fill materials) is the mass per unit volume of a material in its placed, settled state. It is measured in tonnes per cubic metre (t/m³).

    You need it to convert supplier quotes (£/tonne) to BoQ units (£/m³):

    Cost per m³ = Cost per tonne × Bulk density (t/m³)

    Bulk density varies significantly between materials. Using the wrong density produces large pricing errors:

    MaterialBulk DensityNote
    Type 1 granular sub-base2.0–2.2 t/m³Compacted. Use 2.1 t/m³ as standard.
    Concrete (C25/30 standard)2.35–2.40 t/m³Reinforced concrete is slightly higher.
    Clay (excavated, bank)1.60–1.80 t/m³Varies with moisture content. 1.7 t/m³ typical.
    Sand/gravel (bedding)1.75–1.90 t/m³Compacted. Use 1.8 t/m³ as standard.
    Bituminous macadam (surfacing)2.30–2.40 t/m³Compacted mix density. Confirm with lab data.
    Imported fill (mixed granular)1.8–2.0 t/m³Use 1.9 t/m³ unless specification states otherwise.
    Loose vs compacted density: Bulk density figures for fill materials are typically for the compacted state (after placing and rolling). You will need to order more tonnes than the net compacted volume suggests — the additional quantity accounts for air voids removed during compaction. A 10–15% extra order is typical for granular fill.

    Worked Example

    Converting a Tonne Price to Cost Per m³

    Problem Type 1 granular sub-base is quoted at £19/t delivered. The specification requires placement to 300 mm compacted depth. Calculate the material cost per m² of sub-base (the BoQ unit is m²) and the material cost per m³.
    Supply rate: £19.00/t
    Bulk density: 2.1 t/m³
    ──────────────────────────────────
    Material cost/m³: £19.00 × 2.1 = £39.90/m³

    Multiply the tonne rate by the bulk density to get cost per compacted m³. This is the base material cost before wastage.

    Base material cost/m³: £39.90
    Wastage allowance: 7.5% (typical for granular fill)

    Material cost/m³ (gross): £39.90 × 1.075 = £42.90/m³

    The wastage factor covers material lost in transit, spillage during spreading, and overbreak beyond the theoretical compacted volume. 5–10% is standard for granular fill. This is your order quantity uplift — not a profit margin.

    Material cost/m³ (gross): £42.90
    × Depth: × 0.300 m
    ─────────────────────────────────────
    Material cost/m²: £12.87/m²

    The BoQ item is measured in m², so you multiply cost/m³ by the specified depth (in metres) to get cost/m². Your full unit rate for this item still needs to add: spreading and compaction (plant and labour), and any testing costs.

    Concept

    Wastage, Losses, and Over-ordering

    Wastage is not a fudge factor — it represents identifiable, quantifiable material losses that occur during a normal construction process. Each material has a different wastage profile:

    MaterialTypical WastageCause
    Ready-mixed concrete 2–5% Over-pour at edges of formwork, laitance, residue in drum.
    Granular fill (Type 1) 5–10% Spread beyond the theoretical edge, contamination from subgrade, trafficking loss.
    Pipe bedding (pea gravel) 8–12% Migration into surrounding soil, irregular trench bottom, handling loss.
    Pipes (uPVC/concrete) 2–4% Breakages during delivery and handling, cut-offs at junctions. Price as a number of extra pipes.
    Reinforcement bar 5–10% Cut-offs from bar lengths, lap lengths, spacer/tie wire.
    Over-ordering vs under-ordering: Ordering short means an emergency top-up delivery at spot price — often 20–30% more than the tender quote and with a delivery charge. It is almost always cheaper to order slightly over and return unused material (if the supplier accepts returns) than to run short.

    A practical rule: apply wastage percentages consistently using your company's standard table, document the percentages used, and review them against actuals after each project.
