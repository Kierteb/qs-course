    Concept

    What Is an All-In Labour Rate?

    When you price labour in a civil tender, you never use the operative's take-home pay as your cost. You use the all-in rate — the total cost to the company of employing one person for one hour.

    The all-in rate is always higher than basic pay because the contractor pays several statutory and contractual obligations on top of wages. These additional costs are real and significant — typically 35–45% above basic pay for a PAYE operative.

    Using basic pay instead of an all-in rate is one of the most dangerous tendering mistakes a new estimator can make. You would be pricing labour at a loss before a single spade hits the ground.

    Key principle: The all-in rate is your cost to employ, not the operative's earnings. These are different numbers.

    All-in rates are calculated for each operative category: general operative, skilled operative, ganger/chargehand, plant operator (with CPCS card). Each category has different base pay and, often, different contractual allowances.

    Concept

    The Components of an All-In Rate

    Every all-in rate is built from the same components. Each must be calculated individually — a rough percentage uplift is not reliable for formal tendering.

    ComponentRateNotes
    Basic pay — CIJC Working Rule Agreement rate for category. Starting point for all calculations.
    Employer's National Insurance 13.8% On earnings above the secondary threshold (~£9,100/yr). Largest single add-on.
    Holiday pay accrual ~12.07% Statutory 5.6 weeks' paid holiday. Accrued as a percentage of pay for hourly workers.
    Pension (auto-enrolment) 3% Minimum employer contribution on qualifying earnings. Can be higher under CIJC.
    CITB levy 0.35% Construction Industry Training Board levy on PAYE payroll. Funds industry training grants.
    Insurance loading ~1–2% Employer's liability insurance, often quoted as a percentage of payroll by insurer.
    Productivity allowance ~2–5% Allowance for unproductive time: tool maintenance, welfare breaks, briefings. Not always shown separately.

    Rates shown are indicative as at 2024–2025. Always verify current CIJC wage rates and statutory rates for your tender date.

    Do not double-count: Some sub-contractor quotes are already all-in. Some plant hire quotes include the operator. Always confirm what is and isn't included before adding further costs.

    Typical All-In Labour Rates — Civil & Groundworks

    These figures represent typical all-in rates for an experienced London/South-East contractor in 2024–2025. Regional variation applies — rates in the North of England may be 10–15% lower.

    Operative CategoryBasic PayAll-In RateNotes
    General Operative (groundworker) £14.50/hr £20–22/hr CIJC Craft Rate or above. Most common category on groundworks sites.
    Skilled Operative / Drain Layer £16.00/hr £22–25/hr CIJC Craft Rate + skill premium. Required for drainage, concrete, and kerb laying.
    Ganger / Chargehand £17.50/hr £24–27/hr Supervisory premium above Craft Rate. Leads a gang of 3–5 operatives.
    Plant Operator (CPCS — Excavator) £18.00/hr £26–30/hr CPCS card holder. Rate varies by machine category — 360° excavator commands higher rate than telehandler.
    Site Manager / Foreman £25–35/hr £38–50/hr Priced in Preliminaries, not unit rates. Staff rather than operatives in many contractors.
    Where does this number come from? Your accounts/payroll team produce a labour burden analysis annually. The estimating department uses this to set their standard all-in rates for the year. Always use your company's actual rates — do not use industry averages uncritically.

    Worked Example

    Building an All-In Rate from Basic Pay

    Problem A groundworks operative has a CIJC basic pay of £14.50/hr. Build the all-in rate using standard statutory add-ons.

    Starting from basic pay of £14.50/hr, apply each component as a percentage:

    Basic pay: £14.50/hr
    + Employer's NI (13.8%): £14.50 × 0.138 = £2.00/hr
    + Holiday pay (12.07%): £14.50 × 0.1207 = £1.75/hr
    + Pension (3%): £14.50 × 0.03 = £0.44/hr
    + CITB levy (0.35%): £14.50 × 0.0035 = £0.05/hr
    + Insurance (1.5%): £14.50 × 0.015 = £0.22/hr

    Each add-on is calculated independently on basic pay. Some contractors calculate NI on total employment cost, but applying percentages to basic pay is the simpler and common estimating approach.

    Basic pay: £14.50
    + NI: £ 2.00
    + Holiday: £ 1.75
    + Pension: £ 0.44
    + CITB: £ 0.05
    + Insurance: £ 0.22
    ─────────────────────────
    Bare all-in: £18.96/hr

    The bare all-in rate excludes any productivity allowance or tool allowance. It's the minimum cost per productive hour worked.

    Bare all-in: £18.96/hr
    + Productivity (3%): £18.96 × 0.03 = £0.57/hr
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────
    All-in rate (rounded): £20.00/hr

    Rounding up slightly (£19.53 → £20.00) provides a small contingency and simplifies gang rate calculations. You might use £20.00–£21.00/hr depending on your company's policy and current tender conditions.

    To check your answer: £20.00 is approximately 38% above basic pay of £14.50. An uplift of 35–45% is the expected range for a PAYE groundworks operative.

    Concept

    Gang Composition and Daily Output

    Civil and groundworks operations are priced by gang: the group of operatives and plant that perform a defined operation together. You price the gang as a unit, then divide by daily output to get cost per BoQ unit.

    The formula is:

    Labour cost per unit = (Gang all-in cost per hour × Hours per day) ÷ Daily output

    Daily output is the key variable. It depends on: ground conditions, trench depth, gang experience, plant type, access constraints, and weather. Always state your output assumption explicitly in your pricing notes.

    OperationTypical GangOutput Benchmark
    Drainage pipe laying (150mm, <1.5m deep) Ganger + 2 operatives 25–35 m/day
    Drainage pipe laying (300mm, 2–3m deep) Ganger + 2 operatives 10–18 m/day
    Kerb laying (precast concrete) 2 operatives 60–80 m/day
    Concrete blinding (hand finish) 3 operatives 40–60 m²/day
    Manhole construction (brick, 2m deep) 2 operatives 0.5–1 nr/day

    These are indicative benchmarks under average conditions. Do not use them as absolutes — always check against site-specific factors and your company's historical data.

    Deep trench penalty: Output halves (or worse) when trench depth exceeds 2 m, due to shoring requirements, spoil handling, and safe working constraints. Always price deep work at a higher rate per metre.

    Worked Example

    Calculating Labour Cost Per Linear Metre of Pipe

    Problem A drainage gang consists of a ganger (£26/hr all-in) and two operatives (£21/hr all-in each). They lay 28 m of 225mm pipe per 8-hour day in 1.2 m deep trenches. Calculate the labour cost per linear metre.
    Ganger: 1 × £26.00 = £26.00/hr
    Operatives: 2 × £21.00 = £42.00/hr
    ────────────────────────────────────
    Gang total: = £68.00/hr

    The gang rate is the sum of all individual all-in rates. This is the cost to the company per hour the gang is working.

    Gang rate: £68.00/hr
    × 8 hrs/day: × 8
    ─────────────────────
    Daily cost: £544.00/day

    This is the total labour cost for the day regardless of output. Even if the gang lays only 20 m instead of 28 m (due to unexpected ground conditions), this cost is still £544.

    Daily cost: £544.00
    ÷ Output: ÷ 28 m
    ─────────────────────
    Labour cost: £19.43/m

    This labour cost per metre is one component of your overall pipe-laying unit rate. You still need to add plant, materials (pipe, bedding), and backfill costs to reach the full BoQ rate.

    If the gang output drops to 20 m/day (difficult ground), the labour cost jumps to £544 ÷ 20 = £27.20/m — a 40% increase for the same gang on the same day. Output risk is the biggest variable in labour pricing.
