    The Six Units Used in a Civil Engineering BoQ

    CESMM4 uses exactly six units of measurement. Knowing which unit applies to which work type is fundamental to reading a BoQ correctly — a misread unit is a pricing error before you've entered a single rate.

    UnitWhat it measuresCommon examples
    mLinear metrePipes, kerbs, road centrelines, fencing, drainage channels
    m²Square metreGeotextile, road surfacing area, formwork face area, topsoil strip
    m³Cubic metreExcavation, concrete, fill, disposal, dredging
    tTonneAggregates, bituminous surfacing, reinforcement (rebar)
    nrNumberManholes, valves, fittings, piles, tree planting, tests
    sumLump sumAttendance on nominated subcontractors, insurance, bonds, demob

    A common mistake: reading m as metres when the item is measured in m³, or confusing m² with m³ for earthworks. Always check the unit column before entering a rate — a rate entered against the wrong unit is immediately wrong by a factor of the item dimension.

    Concept

    Cubic Metres in Practice — Excavation, Concrete, and Fill

    The cubic metre (m³) is the most used unit in civil and groundworks BoQs. Understanding exactly what is measured — and what is not — prevents systematic pricing errors.

    The golden rule: everything is measured NET. The BoQ quantity represents the theoretical volume of the permanent work as designed, in its final position. No allowance is made for bulking, compaction, over-excavation, or waste. Those factors go into your rate, not the quantity.

    Work TypeWhat is measuredWhat goes in the rate
    Excavation Net void to be excavated: length × width × depth to specified lines Bulking factor, extra for over-dig, disposal transport
    Concrete Net volume of finished concrete element: to formwork dimensions Wastage (typically 5%), pump cost, pour sequence
    Fill Net volume of compacted fill in final position Compaction shrinkage (extra tonnes to order), layer thickness constraints

    Practical example: a BoQ shows 150 m³ of excavation. That is the net hole. Your rate must include the cost of removing ~188 m³ of loose clay (at 25% bulking) plus disposal. The BoQ quantity never changes — your rate absorbs the difference.

    Key Numbers

    Bulking and Compaction Factors — Clay, Granular, Made Ground

    When soil or aggregate is disturbed, its volume changes. These factors must be built into your rate calculations for excavation, disposal, and fill.

    MaterialFactorTeaching figure
    Clay excavation (bulking) +20–30% Use 25% for standard tender calculations
    Granular fill (compaction shrinkage) −10–15% You need ~12% more tonnes than the net m³
    Rock excavation (bulking) +30–50% Highly variable — use higher end and flag as risk
    Made ground Highly variable Treat as a risk item — always allowance for worst case

    Indicative UK 2025 market rates — verify locally before use in any live tender.

    Important: Always state the material type alongside a bulking factor. "25% bulking" is valid for typical cohesive clay, not for sandy soils (which bulk less) or rock (which bulks more). When ground conditions are uncertain, allow the higher end of the range and flag it as a pricing assumption in your tender.

    Worked Example

    Converting m³ to Tonnes — Aggregates, Soil, Concrete

    Problem A BoQ contains 85 m³ of Type 1 MOT sub-base fill and 40 m³ of C25 concrete. Calculate: (a) how many tonnes of Type 1 to order, and (b) the weight of concrete for pump specification.

    Before converting, you need the bulk density for each material:

    Type 1 MOT sub-base: ~1.95 t/m³ (compacted)
    C25 ready-mix concrete: ~2.40 t/m³

    Formula: Tonnes = Volume (m³) × Bulk Density (t/m³)

    These densities are for material in its final, compacted or hardened state. For ordering purposes, also add the compaction allowance (see Step 2).

    The BoQ shows 85 m³ net. But when granular fill is placed and compacted, it shrinks by ~12%. So you need to order more than 85 m³:

    Bulk volume to order = 85 m³ ÷ (1 − 0.12) = 85 ÷ 0.88 = 96.6 m³

    Convert to tonnes:
    96.6 m³ × 1.95 t/m³ = 188.4 tonnes

    Round up to the nearest practical load — typically 20t per vehicle. So order 200 tonnes (10 loads). The extra 11.6 tonnes gives a small buffer for wastage and consolidation.

    For the 40 m³ concrete pour — the pump supplier needs to know the load on any temporary structures:

    40 m³ × 2.40 t/m³ = 96 tonnes

    This matters for: temporary works design (formwork props, falsework), pump truck positioning (ground-bearing pressure), and welfare/access planning around the pour zone. On a £2,500 pump day, getting the weight wrong can stop the pour.

    Note: the BoQ quantity of 40 m³ does not change. You order ~42 m³ (5% wastage) but price the rate for 40 m³. Wastage is in the rate, not the quantity.

    Checking BoQ Quantities Against Drawings — A Sanity Check Method

    Before pricing any major section, spend 20 minutes back-calculating key quantities from the drawings. You are looking for order-of-magnitude errors — not the final precise figure, but enough to know if the BoQ quantity is credible.

    The method:

    • Pick 2–3 high-value items (earthworks, drainage, concrete slab)
    • Measure or scale the key dimensions from the drawings
    • Calculate your own rough quantity (length × width × depth, etc.)
    • Compare to the BoQ figure — a ±10% difference is normal; ±30% or more warrants a query

    If your check quantity differs significantly: Raise a tender query. State clearly what you have measured and the BoQ figure shown. The engineer will either confirm the quantity or issue an erratum. Do not silently price a quantity you believe to be wrong — if it's understated and the project goes ahead on that quantity, you may have limited contractual recourse.

    This sanity check takes less than an hour for most civil BoQs and can prevent a very expensive mistake.
