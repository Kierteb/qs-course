    Concept

    What CESMM4 Is and Why It Exists

    The Civil Engineering Standard Method of Measurement, 4th Edition (CESMM4) is published by the Institution of Civil Engineers. It is the standard document that defines how civil engineering work is measured, described, and included in a Bill of Quantities.

    Before CESMM4, every client and engineer used different measurement rules. One project might measure pipe runs net; another might allow for joints and overlaps. Comparing tenders was difficult and disputes were common. CESMM4 creates a common language: all contractors pricing the same BoQ are working to the same rules.

    CESMM4 is not a pricing manual — it does not tell you what a rate should be. It tells you what to measure, what unit to use, and critically, what is included in a rate even when it isn't explicitly stated. That last point — coverage rules — is where most pricing errors occur.

    Concept

    The Structure of a BoQ — Sections, Items, Units, Quantities

    A civil BoQ is organised into sections that correspond to CESMM4 classes. Each section groups related work — all earthworks together, all drainage together, all concrete together. This mirrors how the contractor will plan and price the job.

    Within each section, individual items describe a discrete unit of work. Each item has:

    • A reference code (e.g. E 4.2.3)
    • A description following the CESMM4 classification hierarchy
    • A unit (m, m², m³, t, nr, or sum)
    • A quantity — pre-calculated by the client's QS
    • A blank rate column — this is what you fill in
    • A blank total column (rate × quantity)

    At the front of the BoQ (or each section) are the preambles — definitions and measurement rules specific to this project. At the back is the General Summary, where all section totals are brought together. Always read the preambles before pricing anything.

    Worked Example

    Decoding a CESMM4 Item Description — Class Code and Three Division Levels

    Problem Decode two items from a civil BoQ: one earthworks item and one drainage item. Identify what each covers and what unit it uses.

    Every CESMM4 item is classified at three levels beneath its class. Together, they build a complete description:

    Class letter
    → First division (type of operation or material)
      → Second division (sub-type, material grade, bore range, etc.)
        → Third division (depth band, size, or further detail)

    The BoQ description is written out in full — the reference code maps to these three levels. You never need to memorise numeric codes; you read the written description.

    BoQ item: "Excavation; material other than topsoil, rock or artificial hard material; maximum depth 1–2 m"

    Class: E — Earthworks
    First division: Excavation (as opposed to filling or disposal)
    Second division: Material other than topsoil, rock or hardstanding
    Third division: Maximum depth band: 1–2 m
    Unit: m³ (volumes of excavation are always measured in m³)

    What is NOT separately stated but IS in the rate (coverage rule):
    disposal of excavated material within 100 m of the excavation

    The depth band is critical for pricing: a 1–2 m excavation costs less per m³ than a 3–4 m excavation. They are separate items in the BoQ precisely because their rates differ.

    BoQ item: "Pipes; uPVC; nominal bore 150 mm; depth 1.5–2 m"

    Class: I — Pipework: Pipes
    First division: uPVC (pipe material)
    Second division: Nominal bore: 150 mm
    Third division: Depth tranche: 1.5–2 m
    Unit: m (pipe runs are always measured in linear metres)

    Coverage — what is in the rate even though not stated:
    excavation, preparation of trench bottom, laying, jointing,
    backfilling and compaction of the trench

    This is the most important coverage rule in drainage pricing. The Class I rate includes all the work of getting the pipe in the ground. Miss it and you underprice by 60–70% of the true cost.

    Concept

    Coverage Rules — The Hidden Work Inside a Rate

    CESMM4 defines coverage rules for every class: work that is deemed included in a rate even though it is not separately described or itemised. This is not hidden in a dishonest sense — it is part of the standard that every QS and contractor is expected to know.

    Coverage rules exist because listing every sub-task as a separate line item would make BoQs unworkably long. Instead, CESMM4 bundles logical combinations of work into single items. A Class I pipe item covers not just the pipe supply and laying, but all the trench work around it.

    Why this matters in practice: If you price a Class I item at £30/m thinking it is just pipe supply and laying, you will lose money. The correct rate needs to cover excavation, bedding, laying, jointing, backfilling, and compaction — because CESMM4 says they are all in the rate. Coverage rules must be checked for every class before you price a single item.

    Class-specific coverage rules are covered in detail in the relevant modules (Module 08 for drainage, Module 07 for earthworks, etc.).

    Concept

    The Preambles Section — What to Look For Before Pricing Anything

    The preambles appear at the front of the BoQ (sometimes at the front of each section). They are not just boilerplate — they define how this particular project is measured, and they can modify the standard CESMM4 coverage rules.

    What to look for in the preambles:

    • Definitions of materials — what does "Type 1" mean on this contract? Does it match the spec?
    • Coverage rule modifications — has the engineer expanded or restricted what's included in a rate?
    • Measurement rules — is excavation measured to the net dimension or to the outside of shoring?
    • Payment terms — what is the assessment date? Are retention rates specified?
    • Specific exclusions — anything explicitly removed from coverage that CESMM4 would normally include

    A contractor who skips the preambles and prices straight from the items is gambling. Changes to coverage rules in the preambles can add or remove significant cost from your rates.

    Common BoQ Errors and Ambiguities — Your Options When Something Looks Wrong

    BoQ errors are more common than they should be. The three most frequent types are:

    1. Quantity errors — The QS has miscalculated a volume or length. If you spot an understated quantity on a high-value item (e.g. the earthworks volume is 30% lower than your takeoff suggests), flag it. Under most civil contracts quantities can be remeasured, but your rate must still be correct.

    2. Missing items — Work that is clearly required by the drawings but has no BoQ item. You cannot price what isn't there. Your options: raise a query and ask for the item to be added, or note it as a risk in your tender and decide whether to absorb it or exclude it explicitly.

    3. Ambiguous descriptions — Items where it isn't clear what is included. This is particularly common at class boundaries: is the geotextile included in the sub-base item, or is it in Class X? Check the preambles, then raise a query if still unclear.

    The process: Log all queries in a formal Tender Queries Register and submit in writing. Document your assumptions. If you receive no answer before the tender deadline, state your assumptions clearly in the Tender Submissions cover letter.
