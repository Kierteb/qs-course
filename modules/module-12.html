    The End-to-End Tender Pricing Process

    Pricing a civil engineering tender is a structured process. Experienced estimators follow the same sequence every time — not because it is rigid, but because skipping steps causes costly errors. Here is the complete sequence you will follow in this module:

    StepActionOutput
    1 Read the BoQ, preambles, and specification Understanding of what is and isn't included in rates; list of queries
    2 Sequence the work — build a programme outline Construction order, resource plan, critical path
    3 Price the measured works — section by section Unit rates for all BoQ items; section totals
    4 Build the Preliminaries section from first principles Class A total; time-related and fixed costs
    5 Apply overhead recovery percentage Recovery of head office costs
    6 Tender adjudication — sense check and set profit Final tender price for submission

    This module takes you through each step, then ends with a full capstone exercise where you price a complete civil and groundworks BoQ from scratch using everything you have learned.

    Concept

    Step 1: Read the BoQ and Preambles Before Pricing Anything

    The single most important rule in tendering: read the preambles before pricing a single rate. The preambles define the contract-specific rules that overlay CESMM4.

    When reading a new tender, work through this checklist:

    • Coverage rules: what is and isn't included in each rate? Has excavation been removed from pipe rates (measured separately in Class E)?
    • Measurement rules: are there project-specific departures from CESMM4 standard? (e.g., trench width measured at actual excavated width, not minimum)
    • Specification references: where does the BoQ description reference the spec? Read those spec clauses — they impose real cost obligations.
    • Provisional sums: which items are provisional and how are they to be valued?
    • Dayworks schedule: what uplift percentages are stated for labour, plant, and materials?
    • Errors and ambiguities: note anything unclear. Raise a query via the ITT process — do not silently assume.
    Queries close before tender. Most ITTs specify a query cut-off date (typically 5–7 working days before tender return). Miss this date and any ambiguities remain your risk. Set a calendar reminder when you receive each tender.

    Concept

    Step 2: Sequence the Work — Price in Construction Order

    BoQ items are listed in CESMM4 class order — not construction order. Class E (Earthworks) comes before Class F (Concrete), which comes before Class R (Roads). But on site, the sequence might be: topsoil strip → bulk dig → drainage → concrete bases → sub-base → surfacing.

    Pricing in construction sequence reveals things that BoQ order hides:

    • Programme duration: sequencing tells you how long the project takes — which sets your prelims duration.
    • Resource clashes: can you run drainage and concrete simultaneously, or does one follow the other? Two concurrent gangs may need additional supervision priced.
    • Critical path: which items must be complete before others can start? Delays on the critical path extend the whole project — and your time-related prelims.
    • Temporary works sequencing: trench support, dewatering, and falsework may be needed at specific points. Missing these is easier when pricing in BoQ order.

    A simple bar chart (even hand-drawn) showing the key operations, their duration, and sequence is an invaluable pricing tool. It forces you to think like a contractor, not a BoQ processor.

    Worked Example

    Step 3: Pricing Measured Works — Earthworks and Drainage

    Problem A BoQ extract has three measured works items. Using the methods from Modules 07 and 08, build the unit rates and section total from first principles.

    BoQ item: "Excavation; material other than topsoil/rock; max depth 1–2m: 950 m³"

    Excavator (13T, £832/day, 160 m³/day): £832 ÷ 160 = £5.20/m³
    Disposal (4 wagons × £550, 200 m³ loose/day):
      Haulage: £2,200 ÷ 200 loose m³ × 1.25 bulk = £13.75/m³ net
    Gate fee (clay 1.7t/m³ × £10/t): £17.00/m³ net
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Unit rate: £5.20 + £13.75 + £17.00 = £35.95/m³
    Section total: 950 × £35.95 = £34,153

    BoQ item: "Pipe, uPVC 225mm nom. bore, depth 1.0–1.5m: 120 m"

    Trench vol/m: 0.60w × 1.40d = 0.840 m³/m
    Excavation: 0.840 × £12/m³ = £10.08/m
    Disposal: 0.840 × £14/m³ = £11.76/m
    Pipe supply: 225mm uPVC = £22.00/m
    Bedding: 0.240 m³/m × £51.48/m³ = £12.36/m
    Backfill: 0.575 m³/m × £15/m³ = £ 8.63/m
    Labour: £19.43/m
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Unit rate: £84.26/m
    Section total: 120 × £84.26 = £10,111

    BoQ item: "Precast concrete manhole, 1200mm int. dia, depth 1.5–2.0m: 3 nr"

    Supply (base + 2 rings + taper + F&C + connections): £1,670
    Excavation + disposal: £146
    Backfill: £ 50
    Labour: £420
    ─────────────────────────────
    Unit rate: £2,286/nr (round to £2,300)
    Section total: 3 × £2,300 = £6,900

    These three items sum to £34,153 + £10,111 + £6,900 = £51,164 for this extract. Every rate was built from first principles — not from memory or rough estimates.

    Worked Example

    Steps 4–6: Prelims, Overheads, and Final Tender Price

    Problem Total measured works: £485,000. Project duration: 18 weeks. Build the prelims, apply OH&P, and arrive at the final tender price.
    Time-related (18 weeks):
    Site agent (£1,600/wk): 18 × £1,600 = £28,800
    Site foreman (£1,400/wk): 18 × £1,400 = £25,200
    Welfare + office (£600/wk): 18 × £600 = £10,800
    Security fence (£350/wk): 18 × £350 = £ 6,300

    Fixed costs:
    Plant mob/demob: £ 3,000
    CAR + PL insurance: £ 5,500
    Signage + set-up: £ 1,500
    ─────────────────────────────────────
    Prelims total: £81,100

    £81,100 is 16.7% of measured works (£485,000) — within the expected 8–18% range. This validates the prelims build-up.

    Measured works: £485,000
    Prelims: £ 81,100
    Sub-total: £566,100

    Overhead (9%): £566,100 × 0.09 = £50,949
    ────────────────────────────────────────────
    Sub-total + OH: £617,049
    Sub-total + OH: £617,049
    Profit (4.5%): £617,049 × 0.045 = £27,767
    ────────────────────────────────────────────
    Tender price: £644,816
    Round to: £645,000

    At adjudication, the team reviewed three key risks: ground conditions (medium — some GI data), services (low — clear drawings), and programme (low — no critical constraints). Risk-adjusted margin: 4.5% accepted. Final tender price: £645,000.

    Concept

    Step 6: Sense-Checking Your Price Before Submission

    Never submit a tender without a sense check. A systematic review takes 30–60 minutes and can catch errors that would cost tens of thousands of pounds on site.

    Benchmark rate review: for each major section, check your all-in rate against expected ranges:

    SectionBenchmark rangeFlag if outside
    Bulk excavation & dispose£20–40/m³<£18 or >£50 — investigate
    150mm uPVC pipe, 1m depth£45–70/m<£40 — likely missing excavation or bedding
    300mm concrete pipe, 2m depth£120–180/m<£100 — missing disposal or bedding cost
    1200mm manhole, 2m deep£1,800–2,800<£1,500 — missing connections or excavation
    Type 1 sub-base 250mm£18–28/m²<£15 — check material density conversion
    Concrete slab C25/30 200mm (all-in)£50–75/m²<£45 — check mesh and formwork included
    Prelims as % of measured works8–18%<8% — build-up is probably incomplete
    Construction Act 1996 — Payment: Check the contract's payment interval (typically monthly). If you have significant upfront costs (mob/demob, establishment), your cashflow is negative in weeks 1–4. This financing cost is real. On contracts over £1M, consider whether your working capital assumptions are correct.

    Concept

    The Construction Act 1996 — Your Payment Rights

    The Housing Grants, Construction and Regeneration Act 1996 (as amended by the Local Democracy, Economic Development and Construction Act 2011) gives contractors four key payment protections. Know them — they protect your cashflow:

    NoticeWhat it isTiming
    Payment Notice Issued by the paying party within 5 days of the payment due date. States the sum due and basis of calculation. Within 5 days of due date
    Default Payment Notice If the payer fails to issue a Payment Notice, the payee (you) may issue their own Default Payment Notice for the sum they consider due. After payer fails to serve
    Pay Less Notice If the payer intends to pay less than the notified sum, they must serve a Pay Less Notice by the prescribed period before the final payment date. Must state the basis and amount of any deduction. Before final date for payment
    Adjudication Either party may refer any dispute to adjudication at any time. Adjudicator must reach a decision within 28 days (extendable to 42 days with referring party's consent). Decision is binding unless overturned by court. Any time
    If no Pay Less Notice is served — the payer must pay the full sum in the Payment Notice by the final date for payment. Non-payment entitles you to suspend work (after 7 days' notice) and refer to adjudication. These are statutory rights — they cannot be contracted out of.
