    Concept

    The Three Elements of Every Concrete Item

    Every in-situ concrete structure — from a blinding layer to a retaining wall — is built from three distinct cost elements that must be priced separately:

    ElementWhat it coversCESMM4 Class
    Formwork Temporary moulds (plywood, steel, proprietary systems) that hold fresh concrete in shape until it cures. Labour-intensive to erect and strike. Class G
    Reinforcement Steel mesh (fabric) or bar (rebar) placed inside the formwork to provide tensile strength. Measured in tonnes (bar) or m² (mesh). Class G
    Concrete Ready-mix supplied by truck, pumped or skip-poured, vibrated and cured. Measured in m³ by strength class. Class F

    Some concrete items use only one or two elements. Blinding uses concrete only (no formwork, no rebar). A ground slab uses concrete and mesh reinforcement, but minimal formwork (just edge shutters). A retaining wall uses all three.

    Never price concrete as "just the m³." A BoQ item for "in-situ concrete, C25/30, walls" includes only the concrete supply — formwork and rebar are separate items. Miss them and you win the contract at a loss.

    Concept

    CESMM4 Classes F and G — Concrete and Ancillaries

    Civil concrete work is split between two CESMM4 classes:

    ClassCoversUnit
    F — In-Situ Concrete The concrete itself — mass concrete, reinforced concrete, blinding, sundry. Described by mix or strength class, location (foundations, walls, slabs, etc.). m³
    G — Concrete Ancillaries Formwork, reinforcement (bar and mesh), joints, finishes, post-tensioning. Each ancillary is a separate item. m², m, t, nr

    In practice, a ground slab BoQ section will contain: one or more Class F items (blinding m³, structural slab m³) and several Class G items (edge formwork m, fabric reinforcement m², expansion joints m). You price all of them.

    Concrete strength classes (EN 206): C10/12 and C16/20 = lean mixes for blinding and non-structural fill. C25/30 = standard structural. C32/40 = higher-strength for sections under heavy load. The notation is characteristic cylinder strength / characteristic cube strength.

    Worked Example

    Pricing a Strip Foundation — Blinding and Structural Pour

    Problem A strip foundation is 600mm wide × 300mm deep × 50m long. It sits on 75mm of C16/20 blinding. Price the two concrete Class F items. Ready-mix C16/20: £120/m³; C25/30: £132/m³. Labour gang (2 concretors at £22/hr all-in): 25 m³/day output. No pump required.
    Blinding width: 0.600m (same as foundation width)
    Blinding depth: 0.075m
    Length: 50m
    Volume: 0.600 × 0.075 × 50 = 2.25 m³
    + 3% wastage: 2.25 × 1.03 = 2.32 m³

    Material cost: 2.32 × £120 = £278/item (≈ £123/m³ incl wastage)

    Blinding is a thin layer — small volume, low cost. Its purpose is a clean level surface, not structure. No reinforcement, no formwork (poured against the formation). Labour: typically included in the pour gang time at low productivity cost.

    Foundation width: 0.600m
    Foundation depth: 0.300m
    Length: 50m
    Volume: 0.600 × 0.300 × 50 = 9.00 m³
    + 3% wastage: 9.00 × 1.03 = 9.27 m³

    Material cost: 9.27 × £132 = £1,224
    Gang: 2 × £22/hr × 8 hrs = £352/day
    Output: 25 m³/day
    Labour: £352 ÷ 25 = £14.08/m³

    Blinding unit rate: £120 + £14.08 = £134/m³
    Foundation unit rate: £132 + £14.08 = £146/m³

    These are your Class F rates for the BoQ. Separately, Class G items (rebar, edge formwork) would be priced. A strip foundation typically requires minimal formwork (trench sides act as mould) but does need rebar or mesh if structurally reinforced.

    Worked Example

    Ground-Bearing Slab — Full Build-Up Priced Out

    Problem A 200mm ground-bearing slab covers 300 m². Build-up: 75mm blinding (C16/20), DPM, A393 mesh reinforcement, 200mm structural slab (C25/30). Price the complete build-up per m² of slab. Rates: C16/20 £120/m³; C25/30 £132/m³; A393 mesh £6.50/m²; DPM £1.20/m²; edge formwork £18/m (allow 70m perimeter). Labour pour gang output: 30 m²/hr for blinding, 25 m²/hr for slab.
    Blinding: 0.075m × £120/m³ × 1.03 = £9.27/m²
    DPM: £1.20/m²
    A393 mesh: £6.50/m² (supply only — fixing labour separate)
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Sub-total: £16.97/m²
    Slab concrete: 0.200m × £132/m³ × 1.03 = £27.19/m²

    Edge formwork: 70m × £18/m ÷ 300 m² = £4.20/m²
    ──────────────────────────────────────────────
    Concrete + formwork sub-total: £31.39/m²

    The edge formwork is a fixed cost (70 linear metres regardless of slab area). Dividing by the slab area converts it to a per-m² contribution. Smaller slabs have proportionally higher formwork costs per m².

    Gang: 3 concretors × £22/hr = £66/hr
    Blinding labour: £66 ÷ 30 m²/hr = £2.20/m²
    Slab labour: £66 ÷ 25 m²/hr = £2.64/m²
    Mesh fixing: allow £1.50/m² (2 operatives, 50 m²/hr)

    Total labour: £2.20 + £2.64 + £1.50 = £6.34/m²

    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Materials: £16.97 + £31.39 = £48.36/m²
    Labour: £6.34/m²
    Total build-up: £54.70/m²

    For a 300 m² slab, the total concrete section cost is approximately £16,400. This excludes: excavation and formation, sub-base below the blinding, and any power-float finishing. Always check the specification — some slabs specify a power-float finish which requires additional plant and labour.

    Concept

    Formwork — Types, Reuse, and Labour Content

    Formwork is the most labour-intensive element of concrete work. The cost depends on: the type of formwork, its complexity, and how many times it can be reused on the same project.

    Formwork TypeBest forReuseLabour
    Plywood sheet on timber frames Walls, foundations, bespoke shapes 3–8 uses High — cut and fix to size
    Proprietary steel/aluminium panels Repetitive walls, columns 50–200+ uses Lower per use — quick assembly
    Stay-in-place permanent formwork RC slabs over voids, bridge decks 1 use (stays in) Low — no striking required
    Earth-formed (no formwork) Blinding, strip foundations in clay N/A Nil — formed against trimmed ground

    The key measure is cost per m² of formed face — the contact area between formwork and fresh concrete. A 5m wall requires formwork to both faces = 2 × 5 × height m² of formed area. The BoQ will describe it as "Formwork, plane vertical surface, height 3–4.5m: X m²."

    Striking time is specified by the engineer — typically 12–48 hours for vertical formwork, depending on concrete gain and ambient temperature. Formwork that must stay longer reduces your reuse rate and may affect programme.

    Worked Example

    Retaining Wall — Why the Cost Per m³ Is Much Higher Than a Slab

    Problem An L-shaped retaining wall is 3m high × 0.3m thick × 20m long. Price the three cost elements: concrete (C25/30), two-sided formwork to wall face, and high-yield bar at 90 kg/m³. Rates: C25/30 £135/m³; formwork £45/m² (both faces); rebar £1,050/t supply and fix.
    Volume: 0.3m × 3.0m × 20m = 18.0 m³
    + 3% wastage: × 1.03 = 18.54 m³

    Concrete cost: 18.54 × £135 = £2,503
    Each face: 3.0m × 20m = 60 m²
    Both faces: 60 × 2 = 120 m²

    Formwork cost: 120 m² × £45/m² = £5,400

    The formwork cost (£5,400) exceeds the concrete cost (£2,503). This is typical for retaining walls and columns — the element is mostly surface, not volume. This is why retaining wall concrete is priced at £280–400/m³ all-in vs £55–80/m³ for a ground slab.

    Rebar: 18.0 m³ × 90 kg/m³ = 1,620 kg = 1.62 t
    Rebar cost: 1.62 t × £1,050/t = £1,701

    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Concrete: £ 2,503
    Formwork: £ 5,400
    Reinforcement: £ 1,701
    ──────────────────────────────────────────
    Total: £ 9,604
    Per m³: £9,604 ÷ 18 = £534/m³

    Compare £534/m³ to the ground slab at ~£55–80/m³ all-in. The difference is formwork. A wall has very high surface area relative to volume. This is why the engineer's choice of wall geometry has a major cost impact — a thinner, taller wall costs more per m³ than a shorter, thicker one even with identical concrete volume.
