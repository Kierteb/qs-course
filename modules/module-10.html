    Concept

    The Road Construction Sequence — Formation to Surface Course

    A road or hardstanding is built from the bottom up in defined layers. Each layer has a specification, thickness, and material class. You price each layer separately in the BoQ.

    LayerMaterialTypical ThicknessPurpose
    1. Formation Prepared subgrade — The in-situ or imported subgrade, trimmed and proof-rolled. No separate material cost but labour and testing.
    2. Capping / Sub-base Type 1 MOT or 6F5 150–350mm Granular layer providing structural support and drainage. Spread, compacted, and tested.
    3. Roadbase Dense Bituminous Macadam (DBM) 60–100mm Structural bituminous layer — carries traffic load. Laid and rolled by specialist gang.
    4. Binder Course DBM / HRA binder 50–80mm Regulating layer, provides a level surface for the wearing course.
    5. Surface Course Hot Rolled Asphalt, SMA, or tarmac 30–50mm The final riding surface — texture, colour, skid resistance. Usually sub-contracted to specialist.
    Not every road needs all five layers. A simple hardstanding may have only sub-base and surface course. A public highway requires the full build-up to adoptable standard. Check the specification drawing before pricing.

    Concept

    CESMM4 Class R — How Roads Are Measured

    Class R (Roads & Paving) covers all road construction except earthworks (Class E) and drainage (Classes I–L). Key measurement rules:

    Item typeUnitDescription convention
    Sub-base layers m³ or m² Specified by material type (Type 1, 6F5) and thickness. m² is common for uniform thickness layers.
    Bituminous layers (roadbase, binder, surface) t or m² Often measured in tonnes or m² at a stated thickness. The description identifies layer, material, and nominal thickness.
    Kerbs and edgings m Linear metres. Description: material (precast concrete, granite, etc.), type (bullnose, half batter), and size.
    Road markings and furniture m, m², nr Lines in m, hatching in m², signs and bollards in nr.
    Sub-base in Class E vs Class R: Some BoQs measure sub-base as a Class E filling item (m³) rather than Class R. The preamble defines which class applies — this affects how you price it. Always check before pricing.

    Roads work is often wholly or partly sub-contracted to specialist surfacing gangs, who quote per tonne laid or per m² at a stated thickness. Your role is to manage the sub-contract, check their output assumptions, and price the package correctly in the tender.

    Worked Example

    Pricing a Sub-Base Layer: Type 1, 250mm Compacted

    Problem Price a Type 1 sub-base layer, 250mm compacted thickness, over 1,500 m² of hardstanding formation. Type 1 delivered: £32/t. Bulk density: 2.1 t/m³. Wastage: 8%. Spreading and compaction (dozer + roller + operator): £2,800/day, output 600 m²/day.
    Volume/m²: 0.250m depth × 1m² = 0.250 m³/m²
    Material rate: £32/t × 2.1 t/m³ = £67.20/m³
    + 8% wastage: £67.20 × 1.08 = £72.58/m³

    Material/m²: 0.250 × £72.58 = £18.14/m²
    Plant + operator daily cost: £2,800/day
    Output: 600 m²/day

    Plant/m²: £2,800 ÷ 600 = £4.67/m²

    This covers the dozer (spreading), vibrating roller (compaction), and operators. Testing (compaction density testing, plate bearing tests) is a separate cost — if required by specification, price separately or include an allowance.

    Material: £18.14/m²
    Spread + compact: £4.67/m²
    ────────────────────────────
    Unit rate: £22.81/m²

    For 1,500 m²: £22.81 × 1,500 = £34,215 for the sub-base layer. This is the m² rate for the BoQ if measured in m². If measured in m³, multiply: £22.81 ÷ 0.250m = £91.24/m³.

    Bituminous Surfacing — Rates, Tonnage, and Laying Outputs

    Bituminous materials are quoted per tonne by the asphalt plant. To build a BoQ rate (usually m² or m²), you need to convert: how many tonnes per m² at the specified thickness?

    Tonnes per m² = Thickness (m) × Density (t/m³)
    MaterialDensityRateTypical ThicknessCost/m²
    DBM Roadbase (60/70 pen) 2.35 t/m³ £95–115/t 80mm £18–22/m²
    DBM Binder Course 2.35 t/m³ £95–115/t 60mm £13–16/m²
    Hot Rolled Asphalt (HRA) 30/14 2.40 t/m³ £115–140/t 40mm £11–13/m²
    Stone Mastic Asphalt (SMA) 10mm 2.40 t/m³ £125–155/t 30mm £9–11/m²

    Bituminous laying gangs typically achieve 300–600 m²/day with a paving machine on open areas. Manual laying in confined areas or small patches: 50–150 m²/day. Output depends on access, layer thickness, and mix temperature constraints.

    Tack coat: A thin emulsion spray applied before each bituminous layer to bond layers together. Cost is typically £0.50–1.50/m² and often missed in tender pricing. The BoQ may include it as a separate item or include it in the layer rate — check the preambles.

    Indicative UK 2025 market rates — verify locally before use in any live tender.

    Worked Example

    Kerb and Channel Rate Build-Up

    Problem Price precast concrete half-batter kerb (125 × 255 mm), supply and lay, bedded on C20 concrete haunching. Rate data: kerb unit supply £5.50/m; concrete haunching C20 £118/m³ (0.05 m³/m of kerb); bedding mortar £0.60/m; laying labour: 2 operatives at £22/hr, 80m/day output.
    Kerb unit: £5.50/m
    Concrete haunch: 0.05 m³/m × £118/m³ = £5.90/m
    Mortar joint: £0.60/m
    ─────────────────────────────────────
    Material total: £12.00/m

    The haunching concrete is the main material cost — it anchors the kerb unit and prevents lateral movement. The volume (0.05 m³/m) assumes a triangular haunch on one side. Kerbs with dual haunching (both sides) double this volume.

    Gang: 2 operatives × £22/hr = £44/hr
    Day: £44 × 8hrs = £352/day
    Output: 80m/day

    Labour: £352 ÷ 80 = £4.40/m
    Materials: £12.00/m
    Labour: £ 4.40/m
    ──────────────────────
    Unit rate: £16.40/m

    This rate covers supply, lay, and haunch for a standard concrete half-batter kerb. Granular bedding, if specified instead of or in addition to mortar, would be a separate Class R item. Road furniture items (gully gratings, drainage channels) are measured separately as nr items.

    Concept

    Road Furniture and Items Commonly Missed

    Roads tenders frequently have under-priced or missed items in three categories. Review every roads BoQ section against this checklist:

    CategoryItems to checkWhy missed
    Edge treatment Kerbs, edgings, channel units, drop kerbs, crossover radii. Each type may be a separate BoQ item. Curvilinear sections (radii) require cutting or specials — missed when pricing only straight runs.
    Road markings White lines, give-way lines, pedestrian crossing markings, arrows, hatching. Thermoplastic or paint. Applied at the end of the job — easy to miss in the initial tender pricing phase.
    Signs and furniture Road signs (nr), bollards (nr), guard rails (m), traffic islands, tactile paving, cycle lane markings. Often buried in Class X (Miscellaneous Works) — not obvious in the Class R section.
    Sub-grade treatment Proof rolling (plant to compact formation), geotextile separator, capping layer where CBR is low. Not always shown on drawings — stated in the specification. Read the spec carefully.
    Adoptable roads carry additional costs: LA inspection fees, S38 legal agreement costs, as-built drawings, independent testing — all are costs to the developer/contractor that may not appear as explicit BoQ items but are real project costs. Consider where these sit in your tender.
