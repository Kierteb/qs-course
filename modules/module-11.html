    Concept

    Why Prelims Are the Most Under-Priced Section in Civil Tendering

    Preliminaries (CESMM4 Class A — General Items) cover the cost of running the site as a whole: welfare, supervision, temporary works, plant mobilisation, insurances, and bonds. They do not directly produce any permanent works.

    Two habits cause chronic under-pricing of prelims:

    • Percentage uplift instead of build-up: applying "10% for prelims" to the measured works total. Prelims costs are time-related and fixed — they do not scale proportionally with measured work value. A small high-value job and a large low-value job can have identical prelims costs.
    • Burying prelims in unit rates: hiding site management cost inside measured work rates. This appears to reduce the prelims line, but distorts unit rates, makes the tender fragile if quantities change, and prevents proper cost control during the project.

    The correct approach is to identify every prelims item, categorise it as fixed (one-off cost regardless of duration) or time-related (weekly/monthly recurring cost × project duration), price each one from first principles, and sum to a prelims total.

    Prelims as % of tender total (industry data): Typical range for groundworks contracts is 8–18% of the measured works total. If your prelims come out below 8%, challenge your build-up — you have probably missed something.

    Concept

    Supervision and Management — Always in Prelims

    Site supervision and management costs are time-related prelims items. They must never be buried in unit rates for measured work. The reason is simple: if the quantity of measured work changes, your supervision cost does not change — it tracks programme duration, not quantity.

    A 20-week contract requires 20 weeks of site agent time whether the earthworks volume is 1,000 m³ or 1,200 m³. Burying the agent cost in your excavation rate creates a problem if earthworks quantities are varied downward — you recover less supervision cost.

    Example calculation: supervision cost for a 16-week contract
    Site agent (SMSTS qualified): £320/day × 5 days × 16 weeks = £25,600
    Site foreman: £280/day × 5 days × 16 weeks = £22,400
    QS (part-time, 2 days/week): £350/day × 2 days × 16 weeks = £11,200
    ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
    Supervision total: £59,200

    This £59,200 is a prelims line item, not a rate-per-m³ component. It is split into weekly time-related amounts when completing the Class A schedule in the BoQ.

    Employer's agents and CDM Coordinators are not your cost — they are employer-side roles. The Principal Contractor pays for their own health and safety and management. Do not confuse client-side roles with contractor-side costs.

    Concept

    CDM 2015 Compliance Costs and Environmental Obligations

    The Construction (Design and Management) Regulations 2015 impose specific duties on the Principal Contractor. These duties have real costs that belong in Preliminaries:

    CDM DutyCost implication
    Construction Phase Plan (CPP) Time cost to prepare and maintain. PC must produce before work starts. Allow site agent time.
    Health & Safety File contribution As-built records, test results, maintenance data for handover. Time cost to compile.
    Induction and briefings All workers must receive a site induction. Ongoing toolbox talks. Allow for downtime.
    Welfare provision (CDM Schedule 2) Sanitary facilities, washing facilities, rest facilities. Statutory — must be provided from day one.
    F10 notification (where applicable) Online submission to HSE. Minimal cost in time. Required when project exceeds 30 working days with >20 simultaneous workers, or 500 person-days.

    Environmental obligations with cost implications:

    • Dust suppression: water bowser or misting system. Required in urban areas and near sensitive receptors. £300–800/week.
    • Noise monitoring: required on some contracts near residential areas. £500–2,000 for monitoring equipment and reporting.
    • Waste transfer notes: time cost to produce, retain, and report. Required for all waste movements.
    • SWMP (Site Waste Management Plan): mandatory for projects over £300,000 in some regions. Time cost to prepare and maintain.

    Worked Example

    Overhead Recovery and Profit Margin — The Final Build-Up

    Problem A tender has measured works totalling £580,000 and prelims of £78,000. The company's overhead recovery rate is 9% and target profit margin is 4%. Calculate the final tender price.
    Measured works: £580,000
    Prelims: £ 78,000
    ─────────────────────────
    Sub-total: £658,000

    This is the direct cost of doing the job. Overhead and profit are applied to this sub-total to reach the tender price submitted to the client.

    Sub-total: £658,000
    Overhead (9%): £658,000 × 0.09 = £59,220
    ──────────────────────────────────────
    Sub-total + OH: £717,220

    The overhead percentage (9%) was calculated from the company's actual annual overhead cost divided by annual turnover. It recovers: head office costs, directors' salaries, company vehicles, IT systems, accountancy, and all other non-site costs.

    Sub-total + OH: £717,220
    Profit (4%): £717,220 × 0.04 = £28,689
    ─────────────────────────────────────────
    Tender price: £745,909
    Round to: £746,000

    The profit percentage is set during tender adjudication — the final meeting between the estimator, commercial manager, and director before the tender is submitted. Profit may be adjusted (up or down) based on: risk assessment, market intelligence, strategic importance of the client, and workload pipeline. A 4% profit on £717,220 base generates £28,689 — before any risk materialises.

    Concept

    Tender Adjudication — Sense-Checking Before You Submit

    Tender adjudication is the final review before submission. It is your last opportunity to catch errors, challenge assumptions, and set the profit margin intelligently. A formal adjudication involves:

    • Rate review: compare every major rate against benchmarks. Flag any rate >20% outside the expected range for investigation.
    • Prelims sense check: confirm prelims as a percentage of measured works is within expected range (8–18%). Below 8% means you've probably missed items.
    • Risk review: identify the three biggest risks in the project. Has each been priced? Or is a contingency allowance needed?
    • Programme check: is the prelims duration (weeks) consistent with the construction programme? If your prelims assume 16 weeks but the programme requires 22, you are underpricing supervision.
    • Competitive intelligence: what do you expect competitors to price? If you are significantly higher, investigate. If significantly lower, investigate.

    The profit margin is the commercial lever at adjudication. In a competitive market, margin may need to be reduced. On a strategically important project, it may be worth pricing at cost to secure it. On a high-risk project, the margin should reflect that risk.

    The Construction Act 1996 and payment: Ensure the tender programme assumes payment in line with the contract's payment mechanism. If the contract specifies monthly payment applications, your cashflow assumes you finance the work for 30–35 days before receiving any payment. On large contracts, this financing cost may need to be priced.
